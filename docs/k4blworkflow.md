# K4BL Document Collection and Conversion Workflow, 2025 (How We Do What We Do)  

K4BL organizes documentary material around enslaved and free Black humanity, stressing individuals as human actors with history, who are also changing history. The primary K4BL dataset of abstracts, keywords, and related document metadata is called the Bias Set, an Airtable spreadsheet of documents selected from the Louisiana Colonial Documents Digitization Project (LCDDP) at the New Orleans Jazz Museum and the Louisiana Historical Center. Our project methods are described below.

![K4BL Summer Workshop 2022](http://lxcprojects.org/k4bl/images/20220813_153452.jpg)  

### TThe Bias Set (Document Collection and Control, Keyword & Abstract Creation)

The name “Bias Set” references the transformative work of Marisa Fuentes, who calls scholars to “read along the bias grain” and confront the violence of slavery's archive as we encounter enslaved and free people of African descent referenced within it (Fuentes, 2016). The Bias Set includes metadata from the LCDDP (dates, language, citations, provenance) in addition to metadata that speak to Black life. That metadata includes:

* keywords
* abstracts that tell the story of enslaved and free Black people in the document
* names of individuals of African descent
* names of individuals of Indigenous descent (where individuals are both, they are listed in both fields
* titles
* abstracts 
* and more....

Our Data Dictionary (stewarded by the Digital Team) offers baseline and iterative definitions for the metadata used in the Bias Set and across the project. These definitions were developed in consultation with the research, community, and editorial teams. Our terminology is responsive, which means definitions are open to revision and re-interpretation as the knowledge base and shared knowledge within the project grows.

**The Initial Meeting (update from 2024)**  

We describe our encounter with the enslaved and free people of African descent in the archive as meetings–encounters with people who have lives, histories, and ways of engaging the world that we hope to relay forward into the present and that we hope to learn from in the future. As researchers, we are meeting them and as historical subjects they are transmitting knowledge to us. We describe our first encounter with these individuals and communities in any given document as the initial meeting and on our project team, the person charged with entering the archive and identifying documents to add to the Bias Set is the Initial Greeter and they are included in the metadata of each transcription and translation.  

The selection criteria for document collection is capacious and guided by K4BL Best Practices, a list of criteria generated by the research, editorial and community teams in a process led by the Managing Editor, and listed in our Workflow Guidance, documentation maintained by the Research Team. 

Criteria for identifying documents for inclusion in the Bias Set 

* The document references Africans and people of African descent, including references to people via names, numbers, nouns, descriptions, pronouns, etc. (i.e. “esclaves marron,” “negro libre,” “trois negres”). 
* The document references topics relevant to Africans and people of African descent during the time period (i.e. the Code Noir, slave trade regulations). 
* The document makes references that relate to a document already in the Bias Set or one that has already been identified as related to Africans and people of African descent, even if it does not contain discrete references. 
* The document references a topic relevant to Black life in Gulf Coast Louisiana that has been identified by the Community Circle and Advisory Board as of urgent need or significant interest. 

The Research Team has discussed how to manage documents that do not have a clear reference to Africans or people of African descent but offers other information relevant to Black life and history, such as geographic information about plantations or swamps used for marronage or resistance movements. We have determined for document selection purposes that references need to be clear and discrete to Black people living or dead, not conceptual. 

Documents without clear or ambiguous references to Africans or people of African descent are added to the “Discard Set,” allowing researchers to track which LCDDP documents have been reviewed by others and which were not.  

The Initial Greeter is a key part of the process. The Initial Greeter must be a senior project editor (i.e. have been with the project longer than two years and attended one summer workshop and with language, translation, transcription and paleography skills).

After identifying documents to add to the Bias Set, the Initial Greeter is responsible for:
* entering metadata on the documents into the Bias Set. This information includes drafting keywords, titles and abstracts that describe the experiences of Black life in the manuscript material. These should be considered preliminary until a Doc Selector and/or the Editor-in-Chief does a final review of the Bias Set entry. 

Document Selectors

Once Bias Set records are generated, Document Selectors select from the Bias Set which records will be brought to the language teams for transcription and translation, and inclusion in the document site (doc site). 

Criteria for transcription and translation builds on the same selection criteria as the initial meeting:

* The document(s) make an urgent reference to African and African-descended life. The story is either untold or well-told and very deservedly so. Without a doubt, the document needs to be made widely accessible as soon as possible. (ex. living through the pandemic encouraged K4BL to focus on transcribing and translating documents referencing African healing practices in Louisiana)
* The document(s) reference African and African-descended life and readers may be familiar with the story from other published scholarly research, but having the primary sources available and accessible will be important to Black public historians of Louisiana. The document needs to be made widely accessible as soon as possible. (ex. the first reference in the colonial documents to the Louisiana dish gumbo)
* The document(s) reference African and African-descended life and a future reader is not likely to be familiar with the people, events, or places described, but what is being described impacts arguments or observations scholars have made, is of interest to descended communities and Black public historians, and/or people broadly interested in history of slavery and the African diaspora (ex. cases of arson, poisoning, and other acts of resistance)
* The document(s) do not reference African and African-descended, but does reference people, places, or events of relevance to an existing set of documents queued or in the process of being transcribed/translated.

Our selection standards are the result of intensive and continued conversation between the Research, Editorial, and Community Teams about centering Black agency and life experiences. This conversation is on-going and in consultation with our KCCAB.

The Document Selector is a key part of the process and so should be a team member who attends Research Team meetings regularly, but does not need to be a project editor. 

Having identified documents and included them in the Bias Set, the Document Selectors:
* are responsible for downloading page image files from the LCDDP to Keywords storage on Dropbox. 
* generating a Google Doc file for each one using the Transcription/Translation template and identifying those documents by their unique ID 
* marking those documents transcription and translation status as “in progress”  

Document Selectors may present 1-3 documents for their language team to begin transcribing and translating. After discussion during power hours, the language teams choose which document(s) to begin to transcribe/translate for inclusion into the digital edition. Slack is the primary mechanism for communication within and between language teams.

### From Bias Set to Transcriptions/Translations (Selection, Arrangements, Transcription, Annotations, Encoding & Metadata Creation)  

Transcribing & Translating

Once the document for transcription/translation has been selected, the language teams review the Bias Set entry for errors and begin drafting the transcription. Transcribers can be any member of the language team with sufficient paleographic skill; these are often Assistant, Associate or Senior editors. When a draft of the transcription is ready, the Translators begin work on the translation. When the transcription and translation are complete, the language team as a whole reviews the transcription and translation to confirm accuracy. This process may take some time as questions raised about the document, script, punctuation or context are analyzed. The language teams may also refer questions to the research team as a whole at Friday meetings. Editors may also confer on documents at weekly editors meetings.  

The finalized transcription/translation is sent to the language team editors for review and batching for monthly Editorial meeting and review by Subject Matter Specialists (SMS).  

After review by the SMS, revised and finalized transcriptions/translations are sent to the Managing Editor for final review. The Managing Editor then sends them to the Editor-in-Chief for final approval. At any point in this process, the transcriptions/translations may return to the editors, the language teams as a whole, or even to the SMS before receiving final approval. The EIC approval is the last step in the research and editorial process. 

The language teams may be working on as many as four or five transcriptions and translations and may add as many as four or five entries into the Bias Set per week. Weekly Editorial and Research Team meetings allow editors of both language teams to review progress on all transcriptions and translations, addressing questions arising.

Conversion to Markdown and Batch Upload  

Once approved, the EIC created Editorial Copies to serve as PDFs for the document and story sites. The EIC also does a final review of the Bias Set entry to confirm any repaired titles and abstracts. The Editorial Copies are sent to Digital Team for markdown conversion and upload. 

To begin the conversion to markdown, the Digital Curation Fellow downloads the updated CSV containing the most recent batch of documents and stories, then refines the data by adding metadata. This metadata enables Wax Facets, the Jekyll template that structures the site, to organize stories into predetermined categories. Currently, those categories are “by keyword,” “by date,” and “by name.” This system follows ADE [minimal guidelines] (https://www.documentaryediting.org/wordpress/?page_id=508), which recommends supplementing plain-text search with additional methods of discovery to enhance access to the edition.  

While the Fellow works on this process, Digital Assistants for Editorial Markdown select the documents they will be responsible for converting. They download the EIC-approved copies from Google Drive, rename the files, and create PDFs to be added to the site. Drafts of the markdown files are first uploaded to the sandbox repository (lxcprojects/k4bl_markdown), where team members can practice, refine their work, and receive two rounds of peer feedback without risk to the live site. Once the drafts have been reviewed and finalized, they are transferred to the official repository (lxcprojects/keywords), where they become part of the published edition.  

This workflow has emerged organically thanks to the planning grant and serves multiple purposes. First, it allows individuals with varied time commitments and levels of experience in the project to participate. Over the planning period, we discovered that there were many individuals reaching out about the project, particularly students seeking research experience and members of the descended community, who wished to assist in either transcribing or translating. Knowing exposure to the process is pedagogical and increases discoverability, we have created space within the process for individuals to participate in either transcribing or translating the documents without needing to be a dedicated member of the core team (i.e. editor), or make programmatic and editorial decisions.[1] Second, we wanted to build in a minimum two-step verification of the abstracts, keywords, transcriptions and translations. 

Creating and reviewing documents as a team allows Initial Greeters, Document Selectors, Transcribers, and Translators (2T, Assistant, Associate, Senior editors) to catch idiosyncrasies of language, syntax, and context (including notaries stylistic choices, repeated paleographic symbology, abbreviations, phraseology) at the level of the manuscript page and make consistent revisions/corrections all the way through to the translation phase. These details only came to light because we have avoided separating research, editorial, and digital processes.[2]

## FOOTNOTES
[1] We first began testing out a non-core Transcription & Translation (2T) team in Spring 2023 with the addition of Ashley Reynaga (Dillard) and Amrish Nair (JHU) to the Spanish Research Team. That has been a huge success and allowed the project to advance progress on the Spanish documentation, connect further to undergraduate research development at a Louisiana HBCU, and support student research and skills-building at the undergraduate and graduate level. As the process, particularly the two-step verification and review, continues to be refined, we envision being able to incorporate volunteer or crowd-sourced material into the process, either through individual volunteers, institutions like the Louisiana Creole Genealogical Society, or hosting “Transcribe-a-thons” to tackle larger batches of material. However, we did not want to engage in that kind of massive endeavor without a process for collecting and reviewing documents, as well as supporting researchers working with the documents.  
[2] This concern was first raised by Associate French Editor Olivia Barnard as a structural concern specific to Louisiana histories of this time period. By compartmentalizing by empire, the French, Spanish, and American periods have been cordoned off from each other, although Black lives have spanned and influenced each. In addition, past documentary projects, like the Works Progress Administration (WPA) abstracts now available in the Louisiana Historical Quarterly (LHQ), have also been created by individuals working in isolation from the full text of the material and not engaging across documents in multiple time periods which caused errors, misreadings, and bias in selection of documents for abstracting. As Barnard noted, “I have to transcribe to create a Bias Set entry, to even know which keywords to add.” While K4BL considered breaking the process down by task, we ultimately decided that a team workflow that involved multiple eyes on a single document would be the best way to ensure accuracy and accountability, as well as mutual learning, engagement, and intellectual growth among researchers themselves.
